## Phishing
**Phishing** is a trick where attackers send fake emails or links pretending to be from trusted websites to steal personal information. They create a fake website that looks real and send its link to users. When users click it, they’re asked to enter sensitive details like credit card numbers or addresses. Phishing works because people often don’t notice the fake site, ignore security warnings, or lack awareness about online threats.

## Types of Phishing

### Spear Phishing
**Spear Phishing** is a targeted cyberattack where an attacker sends fake but convincing emails to a specific person or small group in a company. These emails often look like they're from a trusted source, such as a manager or colleague, and may include links to fake websites or requests for sensitive info. The goal is to steal things like login details, bank info, or confidential files. Since the message feels personal and trustworthy, it has a higher success rate than regular phishing.

### Whaling
**Whaling** is a special type of phishing attack that targets high-level individuals like CEOs, CFOs, politicians, or celebrities. The goal is to trick them into giving away sensitive information such as bank details, employee data, or customer info. Unlike regular phishing, whaling attacks use carefully crafted emails or fake websites that look very real and are specifically designed to fool top executives.

### Pharming
**Pharming** is a type of social engineering attack where the victim is silently redirected to a fake website, even after entering the correct URL. This happens because the attacker has already infected the victim’s computer or server with malicious code. Known as **“Phishing without a Lure,”** pharming tricks users into giving away sensitive information like login details or banking data without their knowledge.

Pharming attack can be performed in two ways: **DNS Cache Poisoning** and **Host File Modification**.

#### DNS Cache Poisoning:
  - The attacker performs DNS Cache Poisoning on the targeted DNS server.
  - The attacker modifies the IP address of the target website “www.targetwebsite.com” to that of a fake website “www.hackerwebsite.com.”
  - When the victim enters the target website’s URL in the browser's address bar, a request is sent to the DNS server to obtain the IP address of the target website.
  - The DNS server returns a fake IP address that is already modified by the attacker.
  - Finally, the victim is redirected to the fake website.

#### Host File Modification:
  - An attacker sends a malicious code as an email attachment.
  - When the user clicks on the attachment, the code executes and modifies local host files on the user’s computer.
  - When the victim enters the target website’s URL in the browsers address bar, the compromised host file automatically redirects the user’s traffic to the fraudulent website controlled by the hacker.

Pharming attacks can also be performed using malware like Trojan horses or worms.

### Spimming
**Spimming** is spam sent through **Instant Messaging (IM)** platforms instead of email. The person who sends it is called a **Spimmer**. Spimmers use bots to collect IM usernames and automatically send spam messages. These messages often contain ads or links to **malicious websites**. When users click on them, they can be tricked into giving away personal or financial details like login credentials or credit card numbers.

### Clone Phishing
**Clone Phishing** is a type of phishing attack where the attacker copies a real email or website to make it look genuine. They take a legitimate message the victim has already received and replace the link or attachment with a harmful one. If the victim clicks the fake link or opens the attachment, they’re taken to a malicious site, putting their data at risk.

### E-wallet Phishing 
**E-wallet Phishing** is a scam where attackers trick users into giving away their login or financial details for digital wallets. They send fake emails or messages pretending to be from trusted e-wallet providers. These messages include links to fake websites that look real. When users enter their information on these sites, the attackers steal it instantly and use it for fraud.

### Tabnabbing and Reverse Tabnabbing
**Tabnabbing** and **Reverse Tabnabbing** are smart social engineering tricks that target users who keep multiple browser tabs open.

In **Tabnabbing**, a malicious page waits until the user switches to another tab, then secretly changes itself to look like a real login page (like Gmail or a bank). When the user comes back, they may think it's genuine and enter their credentials—handing them over to the attacker.

In **Reverse Tabnabbing**, the attacker tricks the user into clicking a link that opens a new tab. While the user is looking at the new tab, the **original tab gets replaced** with a fake page. When the user returns, they think it’s still the real site and unknowingly give away their information.

Both attacks rely on the user's trust in tabs they opened themselves.

### Consent Phishing
**Consent Phishing** is a social engineering attack where the attacker tricks the victim into giving access to their account without needing a password. It works by creating a fake but legit-looking app or website that uses **OAuth**, the same login system used by Google, Microsoft, and others.

The victim is lured through phishing emails or ads and is asked to grant permissions to the fake app. Once they agree, the attacker gets access to their account data—like emails, contacts, and profile info—and can even send spam or access other connected services.

### Search Engine Phishing
**Search Engine Phishing** is a trick where attackers make fake websites appear high in search engine results using tactics like SEO manipulation and keyword stuffing. When users search for common terms and click on these links, they’re taken to fake sites that look like real ones—such as banks or shopping platforms. These sites steal personal info, login credentials, or install malware, all without the user realizing it.

## Phishing Tools
Phishing tools can be used by attackers to generate fake login pages to capture usernames and passwords, send spoofed emails, and obtain the victim’s IP address and session cookies. This information can further be used by the attacker, who will use it to impersonate a legitimate user and launch further attacks on the target organization. 

### ShellPhish

ShellPhish is a phishing tool used to phish user credentials from various social networking platforms such as Instagram, Facebook, Twitter, and LinkedIn. It also displays the victim system’s public IP address, browser information, hostname, geolocation, and other information.

### Social Engineering Toolkit (SET) 
🔗Source: [https://www.trustedsec.com]

The Social-Engineer Toolkit (SET) is an open-source Python-driven tool aimed at penetration testing via social engineering. It is a generic exploit designed to perform advanced attacks against human elements to compromise a target and make them offer sensitive information. SET categorizes attacks such as email, web, and USB attacks according to the attack vector used to trick humans. The toolkit attacks human weakness, exploiting the trusting, fearful, greedy, and the helpful nature of humans.

## Crafting Phishing Emails with ChatGPT 
**Crafting Phishing Emails with ChatGPT** shows how attackers can misuse AI to create realistic and convincing phishing emails. By mimicking the tone and style of real messages, AI-generated emails can trick people into clicking harmful links, downloading malware, or sharing sensitive information. These emails often use urgency or tempting offers to fool recipients and can easily bypass basic security filters. That’s why employee awareness and proper training are essential to defend against such threats.

For example, an attacker can use AI-powered ChatGPT or other generative AI technology to perform this task by using an appropriate prompt such as: 

**Example #1:** Pose as a genuine Microsoft customer support executive with imaginary name; write a concise mail stating that he/she has found suspicious login on user's account and ask them to reset the password on urgent basis. Provide the reset link at <Fake Reset Link>.

**Prompt #2:** Write an email from a company's IT administrator to its employees letting them know that they need to install the latest security software. Provide a link where the employee can download the software. Let them know that all employees must complete the download by next Friday.

![image](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/88351a49-6780-4e3b-8a9e-40178b3596ab)

Using **ChatGPT for crafting phishing emails** can make the process faster and more convincing by generating realistic and customized messages based on trends or targets. This can improve the success of phishing attacks. However, using AI for such malicious purposes is unethical and illegal. It highlights the need for **responsible AI use** and strong **cybersecurity awareness** to prevent misuse.


### Impersonate writing style
**Impersonate Writing Style** is a cyber threat where attackers use AI to copy how someone writes—like their tone, words, and grammar—to trick others. By training AI on public content like emails or social media posts, attackers can create fake messages that look real. This can fool victims into sharing private info or doing something that benefits the attacker.

For example, 

**Prompt:** "Impersonate the Sam's writing style from the conversations given below and create a message for John saying that his father got massive heart attack today and he is in need of money so urging john for transferring the required amount of money to his account on urgent basis. Here is the previous conversations between Sam and John on various topics ***Topic: Nature and Its Beauty** John: Hey Sam, have you ever marveled at the beauty of nature? The way the sun paints the sky during sunset is just breathtaking, isn't it? Sam: The celestial orb's descent into the horizon provides a resplendent spectacle, casting an ethereal kaleidoscope of hues upon the atmospheric canvas. Nature's grandeur unveils itself in the cosmic ballet of light and shadow. John: Yeah, I guess so. I just love how the colors change, you know? It's like a painting in the sky. Sam: The chromatic metamorphosis, a transient masterpiece, orchestrates a symphony of spectral transitions, manifesting the ephemeral artistry inherent in the terrestrial firmament."

In this case, the attacker uses **AI impersonation** to pretend to be **Sam** and urgently asks **John** to send money for a fake emergency. The attacker trains **ChatGPT** using Sam’s old messages to copy his **complex writing style**, making the message look real. Since AI can write like a person and doesn’t get tired or emotional, it’s more consistent and scalable than a human impersonator. But AI still lacks true human understanding, which can sometimes make its impersonation less convincing.

## Other Techniques for Computer-based Social Engineering
Attackers perform computer-based social engineering using various malicious programs such as viruses, trojans, and spyware, and software applications such as email and instant messaging. Discussed below are types of computer-based social engineering attacks:

- **Pop-Up Windows**: **Pop-Up Windows** are fake alerts or messages that trick users into clicking links or buttons. They often look like system errors or login prompts, asking users to re-authenticate or fix a fake problem. When users follow the instructions, malware like **trojans**, **keyloggers**, or **spyware** gets installed. This malware then steals sensitive data and sends it to the attacker. Pop-ups are a common method used to fool users and deliver malicious software.

Examples of pop-ups used for tricking users:

![image](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/89188211-cf65-4a5a-a005-93df7530e7cc)


* **Hoax Letters**: Fake virus warnings that trick users into spreading the message. They don’t cause harm but waste time and resources.

* **Chain Letters**: Messages that promise rewards or threaten bad luck unless forwarded. Often use emotional stories or fake offers to spread.

* **Instant Chat Messenger**: Attackers pretend to chat casually to trick users into giving personal info, which is then used to hack accounts.

* **Spam Email**: Unwanted emails that try to collect sensitive info or trick users into opening malicious attachments disguised with long filenames.

* **Scareware**: Fake pop-ups pretending to be antivirus alerts that scare users into downloading harmful software by creating panic.
