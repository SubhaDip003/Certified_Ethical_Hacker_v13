Attackers may implement various cryptography attacks to evade the security of a cryptographic system by exploiting vulnerabilities in code, ciphers, cryptographic protocols, or key management schemes. This process is known as cryptanalysis. <br>
Cryptanalysis is the study of ciphers, ciphertext, or cryptosystems with the ability to identify vulnerabilities in them and thus extract plaintext from ciphertext even if the cryptographic key or algorithm used to encrypt the plaintext is unknown. <br>
This section deals with various cryptography attacks that an attacker performs to compromise cryptographic systems as well as various cryptanalysis techniques and tools that help in breaching cryptographic security.

## Cryptanalysis Methods 
- **Linear Cryptanalysis** <br>
  Linear cryptanalysis is a method used to analyze and attack block ciphers by finding mathematical relationships between the plaintext, ciphertext, and secret key. It was developed by Mitsuru Matsui and is considered one of the most important known-plaintext attacks in modern cryptography. The idea is to approximate how a cipher works using linear equations that involve XOR operations between certain bits of the plaintext, ciphertext, and key.

By studying a large number of plaintext and ciphertext pairs, the attacker looks for patterns where these equations hold true more often than by random chance. These patterns help reveal partial information about the secret key. For example, an equation like **P1 ‚äē P3 ‚äē C1 = K2** means that by comparing the first and third bits of the plaintext with the first bit of the ciphertext, one can make guesses about the second bit of the key.

You can use this method to gradually recover the key that was used. After deriving such equations for each bit, you will have an equation of the form: <br>
**Pi1 ‚äē Pi2 ‚äē . . . ‚äē Cj1 ‚äē Cj2 ‚äē . . . = Kk1 ‚äē Kk2 ‚äē. . .**

Once enough of these linear relationships are found, Matsui‚Äôs Algorithm 2 is used to test different partial key values. The algorithm counts how often the equation holds true across all known data. The key values that deviate most from random probability are considered the most likely to be correct. Although linear cryptanalysis can significantly reduce the time needed to break ciphers like DES compared to brute force, it still requires an extremely large amount of plaintext‚Äďciphertext data, making it impractical in many real-world cases. Nonetheless, it remains an important technique for understanding and testing the strength of encryption algorithms.

- **Differential Cryptanalysis** <br>
  Differential cryptanalysis is a form of cryptanalysis applicable to symmetric-key algorithms. It was invented by Eli Biham and Adi Shamir. Essentially, it is the examination of differences in input and how that affects the resultant difference in the output. It originally worked only with chosen plaintext. It can also work with known plaintext and ciphertext.

- **Integral Cryptanalysis** <br>
  Integral cryptanalysis is a method used to analyze block ciphers, especially those built on substitution-permutation networks. It was introduced by Lars Knudsen as an extension of differential cryptanalysis. Instead of studying just pairs of inputs that differ by one bit, integral cryptanalysis keeps some bits fixed and changes the others through all possible values. By observing how these changes affect the cipher‚Äôs output, attackers can find patterns that reveal weaknesses in the encryption process.

- **Quantum Cryptanalysis** <br>
  Quantum cryptanalysis is the process of cracking cryptographic algorithms using a quantum computer. Attackers can use Shor‚Äôs quantum factoring algorithm on public-key cryptographic algorithms such as RSA and Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman (ECDH) to find the factors of large numbers in polynomial time and Grover‚Äôs quantum search algorithm to make brute-force key search faster for block ciphers (AES) or hash functions (SHA). <br>
  To perform cryptanalysis, attackers must obtain the encrypted content, and the process requires significant time and quantum resources. Given below are the resources required to conduct cryptanalysis.
  - **Circuit Width:** Specifies how many quantum bits or qubits are required in a time step
  - **Circuit Depth:** Specifies the time steps required for a circuit
  - **Number of Gates:** Specifies how many quantum gates are implemented in a circuit
  - **Number of T-Gates:** Specifies how many T-gates are implemented in a circuit
  - **T-Depth:** Specifies the time steps required for a T-gate
  - **MAXDEPTH:** Specifies the maximum depth of a circuit (e.g., 240,264, or 296)
