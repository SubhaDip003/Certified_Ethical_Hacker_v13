# 🕵️‍♂️ Cryptanalysis
Attackers may implement various cryptography attacks to evade the security of a cryptographic system by exploiting vulnerabilities in code, ciphers, cryptographic protocols, or key management schemes. This process is known as cryptanalysis. <br>
Cryptanalysis is the study of ciphers, ciphertext, or cryptosystems with the ability to identify vulnerabilities in them and thus extract plaintext from ciphertext even if the cryptographic key or algorithm used to encrypt the plaintext is unknown. <br>
This section deals with various cryptography attacks that an attacker performs to compromise cryptographic systems as well as various cryptanalysis techniques and tools that help in breaching cryptographic security. 

---
## Cryptanalysis Methods 
- **Linear Cryptanalysis** <br>
  Linear cryptanalysis is a method used to analyze and attack block ciphers by finding mathematical relationships between the plaintext, ciphertext, and secret key. It was developed by Mitsuru Matsui and is considered one of the most important known-plaintext attacks in modern cryptography. The idea is to approximate how a cipher works using linear equations that involve XOR operations between certain bits of the plaintext, ciphertext, and key.

By studying a large number of plaintext and ciphertext pairs, the attacker looks for patterns where these equations hold true more often than by random chance. These patterns help reveal partial information about the secret key. For example, an equation like **P1 ⊕ P3 ⊕ C1 = K2** means that by comparing the first and third bits of the plaintext with the first bit of the ciphertext, one can make guesses about the second bit of the key.

You can use this method to gradually recover the key that was used. After deriving such equations for each bit, you will have an equation of the form: <br>
**Pi1 ⊕ Pi2 ⊕ . . . ⊕ Cj1 ⊕ Cj2 ⊕ . . . = Kk1 ⊕ Kk2 ⊕. . .**

Once enough of these linear relationships are found, Matsui’s Algorithm 2 is used to test different partial key values. The algorithm counts how often the equation holds true across all known data. The key values that deviate most from random probability are considered the most likely to be correct. Although linear cryptanalysis can significantly reduce the time needed to break ciphers like DES compared to brute force, it still requires an extremely large amount of plaintext–ciphertext data, making it impractical in many real-world cases. Nonetheless, it remains an important technique for understanding and testing the strength of encryption algorithms.

- **Differential Cryptanalysis** <br>
  Differential cryptanalysis is a form of cryptanalysis applicable to symmetric-key algorithms. It was invented by Eli Biham and Adi Shamir. Essentially, it is the examination of differences in input and how that affects the resultant difference in the output. It originally worked only with chosen plaintext. It can also work with known plaintext and ciphertext.

- **Integral Cryptanalysis** <br>
  Integral cryptanalysis is a method used to analyze block ciphers, especially those built on substitution-permutation networks. It was introduced by Lars Knudsen as an extension of differential cryptanalysis. Instead of studying just pairs of inputs that differ by one bit, integral cryptanalysis keeps some bits fixed and changes the others through all possible values. By observing how these changes affect the cipher’s output, attackers can find patterns that reveal weaknesses in the encryption process.

- **Quantum Cryptanalysis** <br>
  Quantum cryptanalysis is the process of cracking cryptographic algorithms using a quantum computer. Attackers can use Shor’s quantum factoring algorithm on public-key cryptographic algorithms such as RSA and Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman (ECDH) to find the factors of large numbers in polynomial time and Grover’s quantum search algorithm to make brute-force key search faster for block ciphers (AES) or hash functions (SHA). <br>
  To perform cryptanalysis, attackers must obtain the encrypted content, and the process requires significant time and quantum resources. Given below are the resources required to conduct cryptanalysis.
  - **Circuit Width:** Specifies how many quantum bits or qubits are required in a time step
  - **Circuit Depth:** Specifies the time steps required for a circuit
  - **Number of Gates:** Specifies how many quantum gates are implemented in a circuit
  - **Number of T-Gates:** Specifies how many T-gates are implemented in a circuit
  - **T-Depth:** Specifies the time steps required for a T-gate
  - **MAXDEPTH:** Specifies the maximum depth of a circuit (e.g., 240,264, or 296)

## Cryptography Attacks 
Attackers conduct cryptography attacks by assuming that the cryptanalyst has access to the encrypted information. A cryptography attack or cryptanalysis involves the study of various principles and methods of decrypting the ciphertext back to the plaintext without knowledge of the key. <br>
The various types of cryptography attacks are as follows: 

- **Ciphertext-only Attack** <br>
  A ciphertext-only attack is when an attacker only has a set of encrypted messages and tries to learn anything about the original plaintexts—or the key—by analyzing those ciphertexts. Because the attacker lacks any plaintext or chosen inputs, this attack is harder than others, but it’s also more likely in practice since ciphertexts are often observable. The attacker looks for patterns, frequency information, or structural weaknesses in the encryption to infer words, formats, or parts of the key; even a small leak (like a guessed word or header) counts as a successful partial break.

- **Adaptive Chosen-plaintext Attack** <br>
  In this type of attack, an attacker has complete access to the plaintext message including its encryption, and he/she can also modify the content of the message by making a series of interactive queries, choosing subsequent plaintext blocks based on the information from the previous encryption queries and functions. To perform this attack, an attacker needs to interact with the encryption device.

- **Chosen-plaintext Attack** <br>
  A chosen plaintext attack is a highly effective type of cryptanalysis attack. In this attack, the attacker obtains the ciphertexts corresponding to a set of plaintexts of his/her own choosing. This allows the attacker to attempt to derive the key used and thus decrypt other messages encrypted with that key. Basically, since the attacker knows the plaintext and the resultant ciphertext, he/she gains many insights into the key used. This technique can be difficult but is not impossible.

- **Related-Key Attack** <br>
  The related-key attack is similar to the chosen plaintext attack, except that the attacker can obtain ciphertexts encrypted under two different keys. This is actually a very useful attack if you can obtain the plaintext and matching ciphertext. The attack requires that the differing keys be closely related, e.g., in a wireless environment where subsequent keys might be derived from previous keys. Then, while the keys are different, they are close. Much like the ciphertext-only attack, this type of attack is most likely only going to yield a partial break.

- **Dictionary Attack** <br>
  In this attack, the attacker constructs a dictionary of plaintext along with its corresponding ciphertext that he/she has analyzed and obtained for a certain period of time. After building the dictionary, if the attacker obtains the ciphertext, he/she uses the already built dictionary to find the corresponding plaintext. Attackers use this technique to decrypt keys, passwords, passphrases, and ciphertext.

- **Known-plaintext Attack** <br>
  In this attack, the only information available to the attacker is some plaintext blocks along with the corresponding ciphertext and algorithm used to encrypt and decrypt the text. Using this information, the key used to generate the ciphertext is deduced so as to decipher other messages. This attack works on block ciphers and is an example of linear cryptanalysis. The known plaintext blocks are generated using a series of intelligent guesses and logic, and not by accessing the plaintext over a channel.

- **Chosen-ciphertext Attack** <br>
  The attacker obtains the plaintexts corresponding to an arbitrary set of ciphertexts of his own choosing. Using this information, the attacker tries to recover the key used to encrypt the plaintext. To perform this attack, the attacker must have access to the communication channel between the sender and the receiver. <br>
  There are two variants of this attack:
  - **Lunchtime or Midnight Attack:** In this attack, the attacker can have access to the system for only a limited amount of time or can access only a few plaintext-ciphertext pairs.
  - **Adaptive Chosen-ciphertext Attack:** In this attack, the attacker selects a series of ciphertexts and then observes the resulting plaintext blocks.

- **Rubber Hose Attack** <br>
  Attackers extract cryptographic secrets (e.g., the password to an encrypted file) from a
person by coercion or torture. In general, people under pressure cannot maintain security, and they will reveal secrets or hidden information. Attackers torture victims to reveal secret keys or passwords used to encrypt the information.

- **Chosen-key Attack** <br>
  In this type of attack, an attacker not only breaks a ciphertext but also breaks into a larger system, which is dependent of that ciphertext. The attacker usually breaks an n-bit key cipher into 2 n/2 operations. Once an attacker breaks the cipher, he gets access to the system, and he can control the whole system, access confidential data, and perform further attacks.

- **Timing Attack** <br>
  It is based on repeatedly measuring the exact execution times of modular exponentiation operations. The attacker tries to break the ciphertext by analyzing the time taken to execute the encryption and decryption algorithm for various inputs. In a computer, the time taken to execute a logical operation may vary based on the input given. An attacker tries to extract the plaintext by giving varying inputs.

- **Man-in-the-Middle Attack** <br>
  This attack is performed against a cryptographic protocol. Here, an attacker intercepts the communication between a client and a server and negotiates the cryptographic parameters. Using this attack, an attacker can decrypt the encrypted content and obtain confidential information such as system passwords. An attacker can also inject commands that can modify the data in transit. The attacker usually performs an MITM attack on public-key cryptosystems where key exchange is required before communication takes place.

## Code Breaking Methodologies 
One can measure the strength of an encryption algorithm using various code breaking techniques, some of which are as follows: 
- **Brute Force** <br>
  Brute-force attacks try every possible key or password until the correct one is found, relying only on computing power rather than clever math or flaws in the algorithm. Their success depends on key length and hardware speed—faster processors test more keys per second—so weak or short keys become practical to break. A real-world example is the successful exhaustive attack against DES, which showed DES’s key size was too small and led to its deprecation.

- **Frequency Analysis** <br>
  Frequency analysis is a basic cryptanalysis technique that counts how often letters or groups of letters appear in a ciphertext and uses those counts to guess the original letters. It relies on the fact that in any language some letters (like “e” in English) and certain words or patterns occur much more often than others; if a symbol appears as frequently as “e” normally does, it’s likely to stand for “e.” Source code is especially vulnerable because keywords and identifiers (for example `return`, `struct`, `#define`) repeat a lot, making mapping easier. Modern, well-designed cryptosystems avoid this weakness by hiding frequency patterns (for example with strong algorithms, proper modes, padding, or randomization), so attackers can’t reliably match ciphertext symbols to common plaintext letters or words.

- **Trickery and Deceit** <br>
  Trickery and deceit require a high level of mathematical and cryptographic skills. It involves the use of social engineering techniques to extract cryptography keys. <br>
  **Example:** It is fairly easy to decrypt an entire message if the user knows some of its content. <br>
  An attacker can use social engineering techniques to trick or bribe someone to encrypt and send a known message, which, when intercepted, could then be easily decrypted using standard cryptanalysis techniques.

- **One-Time Pad** <br>
  One can crack any cipher if provided with sufficient time and resources. However, there is an exception called a one-time pad, which users assume to be unbreakable even with infinite resources. <br>
  A one-time pad mostly contains a non-repeating set of letters or numbers, which the system chooses randomly. The user writes them on small sheets of paper and then pastes them together in a pad.

  **Example of One-time pad usage:** The sender encrypts only one plaintext character using each key letter on the pad, and the receiver decrypts each letter of the ciphertext using an identical pad. Once the letter uses a page, he or she tears it off the pad and securely discards it; hence, the name one-time pad. <br>
  **Drawback:** The key length is the same as that of the message, thus making it impossible to encrypt and send large messages.

## Brute-Force Attack
A brute-force attack is when an attacker tries every possible key until the right one is found. Cryptographic systems rely on secret keys to turn readable data into scrambled ciphertext, and if someone guesses that key they can read the data. To stop this, keys are randomly generated and made long (for example, 128-bit keys are currently considered very strong), which makes the number of possible keys astronomically large. Because trying every key needs huge computing power and a lot of time, brute-force attacks are usually impractical unless the key is short, poorly chosen, or the attacker has massive resources. In short: longer, random keys make brute-force attacks effectively impossible in practice; shorter keys make them feasible.

The difficulty of a brute-force attack depends on various factors, such as
- The length of the key
- The number of possible values each component of the key can have
- The time it takes to attempt each key
- If there is any mechanism that locks the attacker out after a certain number of failed attempts

For example, if a system could brute-force a DES 56-bit key in one second, then for an AES 128-bit key, it takes approximately 149 trillion years. To perform a brute-force attack, the attacker needs double the time for every additional bit of key length; the reason is that the number of keys doubles with an increase of one bit.

However, a brute-force attack is more likely to achieve results.
