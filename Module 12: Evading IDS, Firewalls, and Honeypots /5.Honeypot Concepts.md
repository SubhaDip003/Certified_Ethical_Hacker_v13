# 🍯 Honeypot Concepts
Honeypots are essential components of cybersecurity strategies, and serve as decoy systems designed to attract and detect malicious activities. Attackers perform honeypot detection using various tools and techniques while attempting to break into a target network. This section discusses various honeypot concepts and techniques used to detect honeypots in a target network.

---
## Honeypot 
**Honeypots** are fake computer systems designed to attract hackers and catch them in the act. Since they have no real purpose or authorized activity, any interaction is likely malicious. Honeypots log attacker actions—like port access or keystrokes—to help detect, study, and prevent future attacks. While they don’t solve specific problems, they are valuable tools for early warnings, research, and improving security. However, they require regular monitoring and maintenance to be effective.

## Types of Honeypots 
Honeypots are classified into the following types based on their design criteria: 

### Low-interaction Honeypots 
**Low-interaction honeypots** are security traps that mimic only basic functions of real systems to detect and log attacks. They don’t fully interact with attackers but record limited information like network scans or worm activity. If an attacker does something unexpected, the honeypot just returns an error.

Examples include:

* **KFSensor**, which simulates vulnerable services to attract hackers and monitor TCP, UDP, and ICMP traffic. It alerts on scans and DoS attacks.
* **Honeytrap**, which listens on requested ports, logs attacker responses as "attack strings," and can follow download commands using **FTP** or **TFTP**. It also tracks HTTP URIs.

These tools help security teams study attack patterns without risking full system compromise.

### Medium-interaction Honeypots
**Medium-interaction honeypots** simulate real operating systems and services to trick attackers and capture more detailed attack data than low-interaction honeypots. They respond to specific commands but don’t run a full OS, which limits risk but still allows for good analysis. However, skilled attackers may notice unusual system behavior and realize it's a trap.

Tools like **Cowrie**, **Honeygrove**, and **Kippo** are examples. **Cowrie** logs brute-force attacks and shell activity by pretending to be a UNIX system (in medium mode) or acting as a proxy (in high mode) to monitor real systems.
